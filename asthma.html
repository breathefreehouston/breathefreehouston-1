What is Asthma?

Asthma is a chronic lung disease that makes it difficult for air to move through the lungs.

What exactly is Asthma? An Overview


Asthma (AZ-ma) is a chronic (long-term) lung disease that inflames and narrows the airways. Asthma causes recurring periods of wheezing (a whistling sound when you breathe), chest tightness, shortness of breath, and coughing. The coughing often occurs at night or early in the morning. Asthma affects people of all ages, but it most often starts during childhood. In the United States, more than 25 million people are known to have asthma. About 7 million of these people are children, meaning there are about 7 million other people in the country like you.

To understand asthma, it helps to know how the airways work. The airways are tubes that carry air into and out of your lungs. People who have asthma have inflamed airways. The inflammation makes the airways swollen and very sensitive. The airways tend to react strongly to certain inhaled substances. When the airways react, the muscles around them tighten. This narrows the airways, causing less air to flow into the lungs. The swelling also can worsen, making the airways even narrower. Cells in the airways might make more mucus than usual. Mucus is a sticky, thick liquid that can further narrow the airways. This chain reaction can result in asthma symptoms. Symptoms can happen each time the airways are inflamed.

Causes

The triggers of Asthma are probably a combination of environmental and genetic factors.
  • Air pollutants and irritants, such as smoke
  • Airborne substances, such as pollen, dust mites, mold spores, pet dander, and cockroach waste
  • Respiratory infections, such as the common cold
  • Physical activity (exercise-induced asthma)
  • Cold air

Asthma: Essential facts

  • Asthma is chronic, meaning it can be lifelong.
  • It is potentially fatal
  • Asthma cannot be cured. However, with the proper care and treatment, one can live a normal and healthy life with asthma.

Symptoms

Symptoms of Asthma can vary, and can occur anytime. My symptoms occur when I'm running and haven't taken my inhaler. I have shortness of breath. Other symptoms include:
  • Shortness of breath
  • Interruptions of sleep pattern caused by shortness of breath, coughing or wheezing
  • A whistling or wheezing sound when exhaling- a common sign in children
  • Coughing or wheezing attacks worsened by a respiratory virus, like the cold or the flu

    Prevention

  • Learn ways to control your asthma.
  • Follow your written asthma action plan.
  • Use medicines as your doctor prescribes.
  • Identify and try to avoid asthma triggers, excluding physical activity.
  • Keep track of your asthma symptoms and level of control.
  • Get regular checkups for your asthma.
