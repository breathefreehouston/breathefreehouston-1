Houston and Asthma

Asthma is the third leading cause of school absenteeism amongst Houston schoolchildren.

After my move to Houston, I have developed severe asthma symptoms. According to Asthma and Allergy Foundation of America, Houston’s poor air quality makes it one of the hardest cities to live in with asthma. This poor air quality is caused by the high ozone and particulate matter and exposure to the industrial pollutants mostly from Houston Ship Channel and its large factory industry. The most pressing trigger; however, is the roadway pollution from Houston’s many highways.

The I-45 Expansion: how will it worsen asthma in kids?

There have been concerns expressed in the community that this pollution will only worsen with the proposed I-45 expansion (North Houston Highway Improvement Project). According to the EPA, exposure to air pollution is the worst within the first 500 feet of a highway, referred to as classified traffic-related air pollution zones, or TRAP zones. 80,000 children in 127 HISD schools are in TRAP zones of the I-45. The children attending schools in TRAP zones will be exposed to harmful air traffic pollution. Children are vulnerable to this dangerous air because their lungs are developing and they breathe the pollutants deeply and rapidly, not only worsening already existing asthma, but contributing to developing asthma in children.

A 2017 study by Rice and the Houston Health Department found that these schools in the TRAP zones (northern parts of Houston) had a rate of ambulance treated asthma attacks five times higher than other HISD schools.

Asthma has adverse effects on a child’s experience in school and is the leading cause of school absenteeism in Houston for a chronic disease. This leads to a vicious cycle of low academic achievement and low student morale.

What HISD can do

HISD already has an effective Air Quality Alert System. However, considering the potentially adverse circumstances, it might be very beneficial for the at-risk schoolchildren if:
  • HISD were to collaborate with Air Alliance Houston and TxDOT to fully understand and raise awareness of the health repercussions of this project
  • For example, it could if these Air Quality Alert protocols were to be revisited and strengthened for these schools at risk

Nurses

Also, many of the TRAP zone schools do not have full time nurses. (See the 2017-2018 School Nurse Roster ) Some schools in TRAP zones are in socioeconomically disadvantaged neighborhoods without adequate access to healthcare. For these kids, a school nurse may be the only health professional they will consistently encounter. It is crucial that a full-time nurse, informed and educated about asthma and its consequences, is added to these TRAP Zones schools to protect these children at risk from growing or worsening their asthma.
